Federal Grand Jury Indicts Minnesota Man in Targeted Attack on State Lawmakers

Federal Response Victims' Impact Public Safety
Vance Luther Boelter, 57, faces federal charges including murder and attempted murder in attacks on Minnesota legislators
Share this article:

A federal grand jury has indicted Vance Luther Boelter, 57, on charges related to the killing of former Minnesota House Speaker Melissa Hortman and her husband, and the attempted murder of State Senator John Hoffman and his wife in what federal prosecutors describe as a premeditated campaign of political violence.

The indictment announced Tuesday includes six federal charges: two counts of stalking, two counts of murder, and two counts of firearm-related crimes. At least two charges carry the possibility of the death penalty, according to Acting U.S. Attorney Joseph Thompson. The final decision on whether to seek capital punishment will rest with U.S. Attorney General Pam Bondi.

Federal Response and Legal Framework

The attacks occurred in the early morning hours of June 14, 2025, when Boelter allegedly posed as a police officer and systematically targeted the homes of Minnesota Democratic lawmakers. Around 2 a.m., he shot State Senator John Hoffman and his wife Yvette at their Champlin residence. Approximately 90 minutes later, at 3:30 a.m., he fatally shot former House Speaker Melissa Hortman and her husband Mark at their Brooklyn Park home.

Federal prosecutors emphasized that Boelter's primary motive was "to go out and murder people," making this a particularly troubling example of targeted political violence. The federal charges allow prosecutors to potentially seek the death penalty, which would not be available under state murder charges.

According to federal prosecutors, Boelter had conducted extensive surveillance and planning, potentially spending months gathering personal information about his targets and purchasing supplies for the attacks. Evidence suggests he visited the homes of other state legislators as well.

"Though I was not shot physically, I will now forever coexist with the PTSD of watching my parents be nearly shot dead in front of me and seeing my life flash before my eyes with a gun in my face. My parents pushed me out of the way that night."
— Hope Hoffman, daughter of surviving victims

Victims' Impact and Community Response

State Senator John Hoffman, who was shot nine times, has been released from intensive care and is continuing his recovery at a rehabilitation facility. His wife Yvette, shot eight times, was released from the hospital on June 19. The physical and emotional toll on the surviving victims and their families remains severe.

Hope Hoffman's statement expressing relief at the indictment highlights the lasting trauma experienced by families of targeted officials. Her description of the psychological impact demonstrates how political violence extends far beyond the immediate victims.

Minnesota Secretary of State Steve Simon, who attended law school with Hortman, described her as "a bright shining light of a human being" and "formidable public servant." The loss has resonated throughout Minnesota's political community, where colleagues remember Hortman's dedication to public service.

"Boelter was heavily armed and dressed in tactical gear with a silicone mask and police-style badge during the attacks. He drove an SUV equipped with flashing emergency lights and a license plate reading 'police.'"
— Federal prosecutors' description of the attacks

Public Safety and Political Security Concerns

The case has raised significant concerns about the safety of elected officials and their families. State and local legislators typically have far less protection than their federal counterparts, making them particularly vulnerable to targeted attacks.

In a handwritten letter found in his abandoned getaway vehicle, Boelter confessed to the shootings and made bizarre claims about being recruited by Minnesota Governor Tim Walz to kill U.S. Senators Amy Klobuchar and Tina Smith. Boelter wrote that he refused this alleged request and intended to expose Walz publicly, highlighting the role of conspiracy theories in motivating political violence.

Public reaction on social media platforms has been notably critical of media coverage, with many commenters arguing that the attacks should be characterized as political assassination rather than simple murder. The discussion reflects broader tensions about political violence and media framing in contemporary American politics.

The attacks occurred during a period of heightened political tensions and have prompted discussions about security measures for state and local elected officials. The systematic nature of Boelter's planning and his ability to impersonate law enforcement raises questions about current security protocols.

Conclusion

The indictment of Vance Boelter represents a significant step in addressing what federal authorities characterize as domestic terrorism targeting elected officials. The case highlights the vulnerability of state legislators and the potential for political rhetoric to inspire violent action against democratic institutions.

As legal proceedings move forward, the case will likely serve as a test of how the justice system handles politically-motivated violence while the affected families and communities continue to grapple with the lasting impact of these calculated attacks. Boelter also faces separate second-degree murder charges in Hennepin County, though the federal case will proceed first.
